Introduction to Key Biomarkers

Systemic inflammation is triggered by the activation of specific signaling molecules known as biomarkersMeasurable substances in the body that indicate a biological state or condition.. These biomarkers are crucial in diagnosing and monitoring systemic inflammation:

  • C-reactive Protein (CRP)
  • Interleukin-6 (IL-6)
  • Tumor Necrosis Factor-alpha (TNF-α)
  • Interleukin-1 beta (IL-1β)

1. C-reactive Protein (CRP)

CRP is an acute-phase protein produced by the liver in response to the acute-phase response, mediated by IL-6. It promotes agglutination, bacterial capsular swelling, phagocytosis, and complement fixation through its calcium-dependent binding to phosphorylcholine. CRP levels rise during systemic inflammation, making it a key marker for identifying inflammatory responses.

2. Interleukin-6 (IL-6)

IL-6 is a pro-inflammatory cytokine with a wide variety of biological functions in immunity, tissue regeneration, and metabolism. It promotes the differentiation of B cells and the production of acute-phase proteins like CRP, making it essential for immune regulation.

3. Tumor Necrosis Factor-alpha (TNF-α)

TNF-α is a central mediator of inflammation, activating pathways like NF-κBA protein complex controlling DNA transcription and cytokine production.. It stimulates the production of other cytokines and amplifies the inflammatory response.

4. Interleukin-1 (IL-1)

IL-1 is a pro-inflammatory cytokine and potent pyrogen. It triggers inflammation by recruiting immune cells and promoting other cytokines like IL-6 and TNF-α. IL-1 also causes fever by acting on the hypothalamus.

Common Features of These Biomarkers

  • Inflammatory Response: All biomarkers play a role in initiating or propagating inflammation.
  • Acute-Phase Response: CRP and cytokines like IL-6 regulate acute-phase proteins.
  • Therapeutic Targets: These biomarkers are potential targets for interventions in inflammatory diseases.
